I began playing with computers back in the dark ages, flipping the levers up and down to program in hex, store data on casette tapes and use a small b&w tv as a monitor. Since that time, computers have always been a part of my life. To my family and friends I have always been the guy to turn for computer issues.

Even though I may not have been in IT as most people would define it, I have always been my own IT department. As an actuary, I used SAS to crunch numbers. Imported the resulting dataset into spreadsheets or databases and created macros to further refine the data. I remember calling up the SAS help desk to ask how to perform a certain calculation, thinking it was a standard procedure. Their reply was that it was not and I was on my own. After a few days of trail and error, I developed the procedure. and was able to finish my product.

While I did take some computer classes in college, I am mostly self-taught, reading many books, attending webinars and taking courses online. Highlighting a few:

  • Networking, to help secure my home & business networks. For example at work, have one network for my Pos machines another for my backoffice machines and a separate one for Public computers.
  • PCI-DSS, in order to maintain adherence to the guidelines.
  • Security, to prevent getting hacked.
  • AWS Cloud Architecture, since I figure in order to help secure the cloud, I should know how it works.

Why Cyber Security

It's the confluence of Risk Management and Computer Security

After closing my businesses down, I began searching for a new career. A friend recommended Cyber Security. As a Risk Manger at heart I looked into the field a bit deeper. I liked the similarities between my traditional Risk Management background and what is needed in Cyber Security. So I started studying for the Comptia Security+ exam, of which a good portion is Risk Managment. The Risk Management theory was not new, however, the terminology used was somewhat different.

Another "Aha moment", was upon reading Measuring and Managing Information Risk: A FAIR Approach (Jack Freund and Jack Jones) and How to Measure Anything in Cybersecurity Risk (Douglas Hubbard and Richard Seiersen). Having spent 18 years performing Property & Casualty Actuarial work, I was very familiar with many of the techniques discussed by the authors. To read my article on the similarities and other articles I've written, click here. My Papers Written

On a side note, it's funny how "Data Science" is the new thing. To me Actuarial Science and Data Science are very much the same.

  • Both analyze insane amounts of data.
  • Both require require a finding at the end.
  • Given the same data, no two analyses will be identical.
